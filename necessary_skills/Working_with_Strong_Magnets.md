# Working with Strong Magnets


Working with strong magnets (both permanent magnets and electromagnets) is dangerous and requires care and attention to detail. Below are some useful tips and bits of information that will help you work safely and efficiently. 

___

## Ferrous Tools
Most tools in the lab (screwdrivers, wrenches, pliers, etc.) are made from ferrous material, which means that _**magnets will stick to them**_. You should plan for this accordingly. For small magnets ($< {3\over 8}''$ in their longest dimension) this is more of annoyance than a problem. However, for large magnets there is a serious risk for injury if care is not taken when handling ferrous tools. Here are some tips for common procedures:

* **Do not use calipers to try to measure the dimensions of permanent magnets**. No matter how good of an idea you think it is, you WILL regret it. Calipers have many sharp edges that become even sharper when your hand is stuck between them and a strong magnet. Use an aluminum or plastic ruler instead, or look up the dimensions on the supplier's website.
* Make a plan for how you will use tools such that they won't come close to magnets.
* Make a plan for how you will remove tools from a magnet when they inevitably get stuck. Pulling directly away from the magnet is rarely the best option, usually it is a good idea to roll or pry the tool away so that you have some mechanical advantage. 
* Though they appear to have a non-ferrous top, many of the tables in the lab have steel framing underneath the table tops. Magnets _will_ be attracted to this framing through the table top.
* Try not to get your wallet, phone, or keys too close to a strong magnet.


___

## Non-Ferrous Tools
We have a pair of aluminum-bronze adjustable wrenches for use around strong magnets. Because they are non-ferrous*, they are delicate and they are expensive (close to $100 each). When using them, please abide by the following procedures:

1. Only use them if you need to. These tools are delicate.
2. Only use them to apply torque in the direction indicated on the wrench. This is indicated by either a sticker or an engraved arrow. 
3. Be sure to tighten the adjustable jaw so that it is snug to the fastener before applying torque
4. Only apply moderate torque. The wrenches are small for a reason.
5. **Put them back in their case in the cabinet when you are done using them.** Do not put them in the toolbox.

\* They are not truly non-ferrous, as they are still slightly attracted to very strong magnets. Feel free to test this at your leisure, but you can be confident that the wrench's attraction to strong magnets won't cause injury while using them. 

As an aside, some stainless steels are less attracted to magnets than ordinary steels, but some aren't. In general, ferritic stainless steels (grades 409, 430, and 439) and martensitic stainless steels (grades 410, 420, and 440) are attracted to magnets, while austenitic stainless steels (grades 304 (18-8) and 316/316L) are much less attracted to magnets. 
___

## Permanent Magnets are Brittle
Most permanent magnets are made from neodymium iron boron (NdFeB) and plated in nickel, then copper, then nickel again (Ni-Cu-Ni). NdFeB is a brittle material and will crack easily. This means you must be careful to avoid dropping magnets or allowing them to get stuck to hard materials quickly. Additionally, the nickel plating, while strong under normal conditions, has a propensity to flake if torn. Typically this happens when a magnet is allowed to stick to a ferrous surface without being slowed down, so avoid that scenario if possible.
